I built a small first-person shooter around a single idea: there is no health bar. Your ammo is your health. Every bullet you fire is a bullet you are no longer protected by, and the only way to heal is to find more ammo. The name, Ammo = Life, is the whole design document.

It started as a constraint exercise. Strip a shooter down to one resource and see what that resource has to carry.

## One number changes the whole feel

In a normal shooter you juggle two meters. Health tells you how close you are to dying. Ammo tells you how long you can keep fighting. They pull in different directions, which is why most games let you top them up separately.

Collapse them into one and the tension moves inward. Now every shot is a decision about survival, not just about damage. Do you spend three rounds to be sure of a kill, or one round and hope? Missing is not just a missed opportunity, it is self-harm. Hoarding ammo and playing safe makes you, literally, healthier.

## What the constraint forced

A few things fell out of the design without me planning them:

- Aggression and caution stopped being separate playstyles. They became the same dial.
- Pickups turned into the core pacing tool. Where I placed ammo was where I placed safety, recovery, and risk all at once.
- Accuracy mattered more than fire rate. A weapon that wastes life on spray is a weapon that kills you.

## The lesson I kept

The build is rough and it was never meant to ship. What stuck with me is how much a single, honestly enforced rule can do. I did not need a stat screen or a tutorial to explain ammo as life. Players felt it the first time they fired into the air and watched their margin shrink.

That is the kind of clarity I now look for in everything I make, in games and outside them. Find the one rule that does the most work, then trust it.
