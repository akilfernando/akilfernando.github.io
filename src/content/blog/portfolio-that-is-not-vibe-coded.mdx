Most developer portfolios now look the same. A dark hero with a gradient, a glassy card or two, a generic sans-serif, a wave of emoji, and a greeting that starts with "Hi, I'm". They are quick to produce and easy to forget. I wanted the opposite: something that reads like it was made on purpose, by a person, and that still loads instantly.

So I set this site like the contents page of a journal instead of a dashboard.

## The constraints came first

Before any code, I wrote down rules and then refused to break them:

- No gradients, no glow, no glass, no blur. Solid color only.
- One accent, a deep cobalt, used structurally rather than decoratively.
- A warm paper background, never pure white.
- Type carries the personality. Whitespace is generous and deliberate.

Constraints like these are what keep a site from drifting into the same template as everyone else. When the easy decorative tricks are off the table, you are forced to make the type, the spacing, and the structure do the work.

## The stack is boring on purpose

The site is built with [Astro](https://astro.build). It ships zero JavaScript by default, so most pages are pure HTML and CSS. The styling is vanilla CSS with custom properties as design tokens. No utility-class soup, no component library, no runtime.

Fonts are self-hosted rather than pulled from a CDN, so there is no third-party call and no layout shift. The whole thing is static output that I can leave alone for years and trust to still work.

## Where the character lives

The signature is the homepage. It is a masthead and a typographic contents list, each entry a title and a one-line blurb, cobalt on hover. Carried across every page is a small mono label and a thin cobalt rule, a quiet wayfinding device that tells you where you are without a heavy nav.

None of this is novel technology. That is the point. The interesting part is the editing: deciding what to leave out. A site that ships almost no JavaScript and says one thing clearly will outlast a site that says nothing loudly.
